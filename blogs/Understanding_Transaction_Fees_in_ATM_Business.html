Understanding Transaction Fees in ATM Business

A comprehensive guide to transaction fee structures, interchange rates, and how to maximize revenue from your ATM operations.

Transaction fees are the lifeblood of your ATM business. Understanding how these fees work, who pays them, and how they're distributed is crucial for maximizing your profitability. This comprehensive guide will break down the complex world of ATM transaction fees.

When a customer uses an ATM, fees may be charged. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) does allow a surcharge to be charged to a customer for transactions at White Label ATMs (WLAs) after the monthly free transaction limit is exceeded. Understanding the fees are essential before getting into ATM Business:

Interchange Fee

This fee is paid by the cardholder's bank (issuing bank) to the ATM owner's bank (acquiring bank or WLA Operator). It compensates for the cost of processing the transaction.

This is the fee that the bank pays to the WLA operator for using their ATM network.

These charges apply to customers after they have exhausted their free monthly transaction limit for off-us ATMs (ATMs of other banks or WLAs).

Fee Distribution Model

In India, when a customer withdraws cash from an ATM, the fee structure and flow of money depend on whether the customer uses their own bank's ATM, another bank's ATM, or a White Label ATM (WLA). The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) regulates the maximum fees and the number of free transactions allowed.

When a customer withdraws money from your ATM, here's how the fees typically flow:

Fee flow for bank-owned ATMs

The flow of fees for bank-owned ATMs involves two primary scenarios:

On-us transaction (customer uses their own bank's ATM)

This is a straightforward transaction between the customer and their bank.

  • Free transactions The customer is allowed five free transactions per month at their own bank's ATMs.
  • Beyond the free limit: If the customer exceeds the free limit, their bank directly levies a fee. As of May 1, 2025, the RBI allows banks to charge up to ‚Çπ23 per transaction beyond the free limit. This fee is transferred to the customer's bank to cover operational costs. .

Off-us transaction (customer uses another bank's ATM)

This transaction involves multiple parties, and an interchange fee is charged between the banks.

  • Customer's bank: The customer's bank is charged an "interchange fee" by the bank that owns the ATM. For example, if an HDFC Bank customer uses an SBI ATM, HDFC Bank must pay SBI an interchange fee.
  • Fee flow: The interchange fee is settled via a payment network like the National Financial Switch (NFS), which is managed by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI).
  • Beyond the free limit: If the customer exceeds their free transaction limit for using other banks' ATMs (three transactions in metro cities, five in non-metro), their bank passes the fee on to the customer.
  • Maximum charge: The maximum amount a customer can be charged is set by the RBI, which as of May 1, 2025, is ‚Çπ23 per transaction.

Fee flow for White Label ATMs (WLAs)

WLAs are owned and operated by non-bank entities authorized by the RBI, such as Tata Communications Payment Solutions (Indicash). They generate revenue from the interchange fees paid by banks.

Sponsor bank:

Each WLA operator has a "sponsor bank" that handles the cash management and settlement processes.

Transaction flow:

  • Step 1: A customer of any bank (e.g., Bank of Baroda) uses a WLA to withdraw cash.
  • Step 2: The WLA operator processes the transaction through a payment network (like NFS) and gets reimbursed the cash by its sponsor bank.
  • Step 3: The WLA's sponsor bank then raises a claim on the customer's bank (Bank of Baroda).
  • Step 4: The customer's bank pays an interchange fee to the WLA via the sponsor bank for facilitating the transaction.

Fee recovery:

The WLA operator gets paid the interchange fee, which covers their operational costs, based on the volume of transactions.

If the customer exceeds their monthly free limit (which also applies to WLAs), their bank charges the fee to the customer to recover the interchange cost.

Factors Influencing Fee Income

Location-Based Income

Your ATM's location significantly impacts the taffic to your ATM:

  • Premium Locations with high footfall: 2000-3000 daily foot fall can increase higher chances
  • Standard Retail: Shopping malls, supermarkets typically has average foot fall
  • Rural Areas: Location where ATMs are on high demand

Competitive Landscape

Research what nearby ATMs are charging. Being significantly higher might drive customers away, while being lower could reduce your profitability unnecessarily.

Cost Management

Reduce your operational costs to increase net revenue:

  • Optimize cash replenishment schedules
  • Negotiate better rates with service providers
  • Implement preventive maintenance to reduce repair costs

Revenue Optimization Tip

Consider offering additional services like mobile recharge, bill payments, or mini-statements. These can generate additional revenue streams without significant additional costs.

Regulatory Considerations

In India, ATM fees are regulated by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI):

  • Banks cannot charge their own customers for using their ATMs
  • Free transaction limits are set for using other banks' ATMs
  • White Label ATM operators have specific fee structure guidelines
  • Transparency in fee disclosure is mandatory

Calculating Your Break-Even Point

Use this formula to determine how many transactions you need to break even:

Break-Even Formula

Monthly Break-Even Transactions = Total Monthly Costs / (Average Revenue Per Transaction)
